The structure beneath the search

A museum search is a funnel: every filter narrows a list toward the one object you already wanted. It never shows how the collection's dimensions relate, or what a filter hides. This does the opposite — it makes the structure visible first.

All counts are computed from the full public-domain working subset (30,000 objects). Facets: department, material, time period, visibility, culture.

The whole, at once

What a search page never lets you see

The Met search shows “1–42 of 535,006” and makes you paginate — you never perceive the whole. Here every object in the subset is one tile, grouped by department then material. The true proportions are unavoidable: a few departments fill most of the collection; most are slivers.

Relationships

How any two dimensions relate

The Met lets you filter by department, then material, then geography — but never shows how they connect. Choose two dimensions; each cell is how many objects share that pair. Darker means more.

Curatorial attention

Where the spotlight actually falls

The Met search offers a single “Highlights” checkbox, but never shows where highlights concentrate. Pick any dimension; each bar is that group, and the coloured sliver is the share flagged as highlight or timeline work. The attention is wildly uneven.

What the filter hides

The empty cells are data too

A search returns only what survives your filters; it never tells you what your choice excluded. Pick a dimension and see, for each value, how much of the collection it leaves out — and, for the pairing you chose above, which combinations simply do not exist.
